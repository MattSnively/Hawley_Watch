## The Statement

After signing HB 567 to repeal Missouri's voter-approved paid sick leave law (Proposition A), Governor Kehoe defended his decision:

> "It was no one in Missouri who brought that bill to the ballot. It was bought and put on our ballot."
>
> — Governor Mike Kehoe, July 2025

*Source: KCTV5 Fact Check, July 22, 2025*

---

## Claim-by-Claim Fact Check

| Claim | Verdict |
|-------|---------|
| "No one in Missouri" brought Proposition A to the ballot | **FALSE** — The campaign was organized by Missourians and supported by Missouri voters who signed the petition and voted for it |
| The measure was "bought and put on our ballot" by outsiders | **MISLEADING** — Some national organizations contributed funding, which is standard for ballot campaigns in every state; the campaign described itself as "Missouri-born, supported by Missourians and passed by Missourians" |
| (Implied) Overturning the vote was therefore justified | **MISLEADING** — Even if outside money was involved, Missouri voters approved the measure at the ballot box; the democratic outcome is the vote, not the funding source |

## Analysis

### Factor 1: Factual Accuracy — 20%

Kehoe's claim contains a kernel of truth buried under a misleading frame.

**What's true:** National labor organizations did contribute funding to the Proposition A campaign. This is standard practice for ballot initiatives across the country — both liberal and conservative groups fund ballot campaigns in other states.

**What's false:** The claim that "no one in Missouri" brought it to the ballot is factually wrong:
- Missouri residents collected the petition signatures
- Missouri voters signed those petitions to put it on the ballot
- Missouri voters approved it at the ballot box
- The campaign organization stated it was "Missouri-born, supported by Missourians and passed by Missourians"

The ballot initiative process in Missouri requires signatures from registered Missouri voters. By definition, Missourians brought it to the ballot. That is how the petition process works.

**Rating: Mostly False**

### Factor 2: Intent to Mislead — 15%

Kehoe's framing serves a specific political purpose: delegitimize a voter-approved law so he can justify overturning it.

The logic chain he's constructing:
1. The law was pushed by outsiders (not Missourians)
2. Therefore it doesn't reflect what Missourians want
3. Therefore repealing it is actually doing what Missourians want
4. Despite the fact that Missourians voted for it

This is a rhetorical sleight of hand. The democratic legitimacy of a ballot measure comes from the vote itself — not from who funded the campaign. If campaign funding invalidated ballot results, every election in America would be illegitimate, since virtually all major campaigns receive some out-of-state money.

Kehoe knows this. He has accepted millions in campaign contributions from PACs and donors with national connections. He does not apply the "out-of-state money = illegitimate" standard to his own campaigns.

**Rating: Willful Spin**

### Factor 3: Context & Cherry-Picking — 20%

Kehoe's statement omits critical context:

| What Kehoe Said | What He Left Out |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Outside interests put it on the ballot | Missouri voters signed the petitions and approved it at the polls |
| (Implied) He's protecting Missouri from outside influence | He accepted $1.25M+ from Rex Sinquefield's American Dream PAC |
| The measure was "bought" | He signed it into law while taking donations from Torch Electronics and Good Day Farms |
| | He rented a charter bus from a lobbyist whose client is owned by a Chinese company — while campaigning on anti-China rhetoric |

The most significant omission: **Missouri voters approved this law.** The people spoke through the democratic process Kehoe claims to be defending, and he overturned their decision while blaming shadowy outsiders.

**Rating: Significant Omission**

## What Proposition A Actually Did

For context, here's what Missouri voters approved — and what Kehoe repealed:

- **Required employers** with 15+ employees to provide 1 hour of paid sick leave per 30 hours worked
- **Capped at** 56 hours (7 days) per year for large employers, 40 hours for small employers
- **Applied to** full-time and part-time workers
- **Passed** at the ballot box by Missouri voters

This is not a radical policy. Paid sick leave laws exist in 15+ states and dozens of cities. The voters of Missouri decided they wanted it. Their governor decided they were wrong.
